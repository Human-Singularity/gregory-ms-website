Since 2021, Gregory-MS has indexed over 42,000 articles, tracked 48 drugs and therapies, and delivered weekly research alerts to a community of researchers and patients who refuse to wait for someone else to connect the dots. The AI models behind the platform have flagged more than 4,400 papers as relevant for multiple sclerosis — within hours of publication, not months.

That track record is no longer just an MS story.

Today we announce the launch of [Brain-Regeneration.com](https://brain-regeneration.com), an open observatory for myelin repair and central nervous system regeneration. It uses the same GregoryAI engine, the same AI filtering, and the same principle that built Gregory-MS: expert-guided curation at machine speed. But the scope now covers every major therapeutic path to brain repair — remyelination, neuroinflammation, stem-cell therapies, cell reprogramming — across multiple sclerosis, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and other neurodegenerative conditions.

### Why expand now{.title}

Remyelination research does not belong exclusively to MS. Neuroinflammation does not stop at one diagnosis. The labs pushing brain repair forward work across disease boundaries, and the tools that serve them should do the same.

Gregory-MS proved the model works. Brain-Regeneration.com applies it to a broader scope in hope of accelerating discovery of a cure, and therapies that bring back what MS stole from us.

### What changes for you{.title}

Nothing you rely on disappears. Your MS-specific alerts have been migrated to the new project, and the observatory categories continue exactly as they are. What changes is the context around them.

Gregory-MS now operates within a broader platform where MS research sits alongside work on cell reprogramming, neuroimmune regulation, and the role of peripheral inflammation in neurodegeneration. That means more opportunities to spot connections that a single-disease lens would miss — a neuroinflammation finding from Parkinson's research that applies to MS, or a cell reprogramming technique developed for stroke recovery that opens a new path for demyelinating diseases.

### The teams shaping the new observatory{.title}

Three research groups have joined as Scientific Curators, defining the scientific scope and curation criteria for their domains.

[REGENERAR](https://regenerar.eu) at the University of Coimbra, led by Prof. Lino Ferreira, focuses on cell reprogramming — using epigenetic editing to turn the brain's own cells into functioning neurons.

[LPJ Lab](https://www.lpjlab.org/), led by Prof. Luca Peruzzotti-Jametti, investigates how cell metabolism shapes brain-immune interactions and new therapeutic avenues for MS and other CNS disorders.

The [CNS, Blood and Peripheral Inflammation Lab](https://imed.ulisboa.pt/labs/central-nervous-system-blood-and-peripheral-inflammation/), led by Prof. Adelaide Fernandes, studies how inflammation drives the emergence and progression of neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative disorders.

### What you can do{.title}

Explore [Brain-Regeneration.com](https://brain-regeneration.com) and [subscribe](https://brain-regeneration.com/subscribe) to alerts in the research areas that matter to you.

Share this with colleagues in adjacent fields — neuroinflammation, neuroimmunology, regenerative medicine. The observatory is built for them too.

If you lead a research group working on brain repair and want to define what the observatory tracks, [get in touch](https://brain-regeneration.com/contact). We are looking for new Scientific Curators to expand the scope into additional research areas.

Five years ago, Gregory-MS started as one patient's attempt to keep up with the science. Today, that work hopes to be a platform that serves everyone working to repair the brain.
